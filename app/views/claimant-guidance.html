If you do not get your money back

If the person or business who owes you money does not pay, you can ask the court to collect payment for you (enforcing a judgment).

Types of enforcement include asking the court to:

  • send a bailiff to their home or business
  • take money from their wages
  • freeze their bank account
  • put a charging order on property they own

You will need to pay a court fee each time you ask the court to use enforcement. For example, if you cannot get money from their wages, you will need to pay another fee to send a bailiff. The court adds this fee to the total debt owed to you, but there is no guarantee that the court will get your money back.

Find out what they can afford to pay

Ask the court to order the person or business who owes you money to attend court to give evidence of their income or spending, for example bills and statements.

The court fee is £55.

If a business owes you money, you can ask for an officer from the company to attend court and give details of its accounts.

You can then decide if and how you want the court to take further action to collect your payment.

Send bailiffs to collect payment

You can ask the court to send bailiffs to collect the money. You’ll need to apply for either a writ of control or a warrant of control depending on how much you are owed.

County court bailiffs enforce warrants, work for the courts, and charge no fees. High court bailiffs enforce writs, work for private companies, and charge fees.

What a bailiff will do

Bailiffs will contact the person or business who owes you money and give them 7 days to pay the debt. After 7 days the bailiff will go to their address to see if there’s anything they own that a bailiff can sell at an auction. If there is, the bailiff will take control of their goods under a Controlled Goods Agreement. The money raised at the auction will go towards paying the debt and bailiff fees.

The bailiff might suggest a monthly payment plan instead of selling their goods. A payment plan can only be set up if you accept the offer.

You may need legal advice if you apply at the High Court.

Warrant of control

You can apply for a warrant of control for a debt of less than £5,000, which includes:

  • your original claim fee
  • the £77 fee to issue the warrant

You can also get a warrant for a debt of more than £5,000 if it’s an agreement regulated under the Consumer Credit Act 1974.

The court fee is £77.

If you made your claim online, you can also request a warrant online.

If you used a paper form to make your claim, you will need to download and fill in request for warrant of control form N323. You must send the form to the court where you made the claim, along with a cheque or postal order for the fee.

Writ of control

You can only apply for a writ of control if you are owed more than £600.

The court fee is £66.

A writ of control must be approved first by the county court that issued your judgment and then by a high court.

If you name a bailiff in your application it is up to you to give the writ to the high court bailiff. Otherwise a high court bailiff in your area will enforce the writ. High court bailiffs charge for their services with the costs being added to the overall debt.

Get money deducted from wages

You can ask the court to send an order to their employer ordering the employer to take money from their wages.

The court fee is £110.

Download and fill in a request for an attachment of earnings order form N337.

Freeze assets or money in an account

You can ask the court to freeze money in their bank, building society or business account. The court will decide if money from the account can be used to pay the debt.

The court fee is £110.

Download and fill in a request for a third party debt order form N349.

Charge the debtor’s land or property

You can ask the court to charge the debtor’s land or property. If the land or property is sold, the debtor must pay this charge before they get their money.

The court fee is £110.

Download and fill in a request for a charging order form N379.
