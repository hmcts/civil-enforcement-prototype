Send bailiffs to collect payment

You can ask the court to send bailiffs to collect the money. You’ll need to apply for either a writ of control or a warrant of control depending on:

  • how much you are owed
  • if it’s an agreement regulated under the Consumer Credit Act 1974

County court bailiffs enforce warrants, work for the courts, and charge no extra fees. High court bailiffs enforce writs, work for private companies, and charge extra fees.

What a bailiff will do

Bailiffs will contact the person or business who owes you money and give them 7 days to pay the debt.

After 7 days the bailiff will go to their address to see if they can get the debt paid in full. If that’s not possible the bailiff will see if they have any possessions that the bailiff can sell at auction.

The bailiff will take control of their goods under a Controlled Goods Agreement. The money raised at the auction will go towards paying the debt and bailiff fees.

The bailiff might suggest a payment plan instead of selling their goods. A payment plan can only be set up if you accept the offer.

You may need legal advice if you apply at the High Court.

Warrant of control

The court can issue a warrant of control if the debt is less than £5,000, which includes:

  • your original claim fee
  • the fee to issue the warrant

You can also get a warrant for a debt of more than £5000 if it’s an agreement regulated under the Consumer Credit Act 1974.

You can apply for a warrant of control:

  • online if you made your claim on [Money Claim Online] (£77)
  • by filling in request for warrant of control N323 and sending the form to the court where you made the claim (£110)

Writ of control

You can only apply for a writ of control if you are owed more than £600.

The court fee is £66.

A writ of control must be approved first by the county court that issued your judgment and then by the High Court.

If you name a bailiff in your application it’s up to you to give the writ to the high court bailiff. Otherwise a high court bailiff in your area will enforce the writ. High court bailiffs charge for their services with the costs being added to the overall debt.
